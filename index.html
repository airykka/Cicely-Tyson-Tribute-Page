Cicely Tyson

A trailblazer whose legacy couldn’t be measured by her Emmys and Tony and Oscar alone, but by the barriers she broke and the dreams she made possible”.
— Barrack Obama

Cicely Tyson. "You made me feel loved and seen and valued in a world where there is still a cloak of invisibility for us dark chocolate girls." -Viola Davis. Photograph: Harpo/PA

Cicely Tyson was an award-winning film, television and stage actress known for playing strong Black leads and refusing to play roles that were demeaning to Black women. The actor has long been considered a force in the entertainment industry, opening doors for many Black professionals to follow.

Tyson had a well-known commitment to community involvement. She co-founded the Dance Theater of Harlem after Martin Luther King Jr.'s assassination, and when a school board in East Orange, New Jersey, wanted to name a performing arts school after her, she only agreed to accept the honor if she could participate in school activities. In addition to attending meetings and events, Tyson even taught a master class at the school.

Unless a piece really said something, I had no interest in it. I have got to know that I have served some purpose here.

– Cicely Tyson

Awards & Honors

1963 - 1974

Tyson became the first African American star of a TV drama in the series East Side/West Side, playing the role of secretary Jane Foster. She also got nominated for an Academy Award for Sounder in 1972 and won two Emmy Awards in 1974 for her role in "The Autobiography of Miss Jane Pittman".

1977 - 1982

She became a member of the Black Filmmakers Hall of Fame and was honored by the Congress of Racial Equality and by the National Council of Negro Women in 1977. In 1980, Cicely received the Golden Plate Award of the American Academy of Achievement and in 1982, she was awarded the Women in Film Crystal Award. The award is given to outstanding women who, through their endurance and the excellence of their work, have helped to expand the role of women within the entertainment industry.

1983 - 1994

In 1983, Cicely Tyson was the lead in The Corn Is Green, a play set in a Welsh mining town. In 1988, she received a Candace Award for Distinguished Service from the National Coalition of 100 Black Women and in 1994, she was awarded her third Emmy in her supporting role as housemaid Castalia in CBS' miniseries television adaptation of the Oldest Living Confederate Widow Tells All.

2010 - 2013

In 2010, Tyson was awarded the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People presented Tyson with its 95th Spingarn Medal — an award given to African Americans who have reached outstanding levels of achievement. In 2011, she appeared in The Help (2011) as maid Constantine Bates, for which she received many awards for being part of the ensemble cast. In 2013, Cicely recieved her Tony Award for best performance by an actress in a leading role in a play for the Broadway show, Horton Foote's The Trip to Bountiful.

2015 - 2017

Cicely was nominated for an Emmy for her guest-starring role in ABC's How to Get Away With Murder in 2015. In the same year, she was also the recipient of the Kennedy Center Honors. The next year, 2016, she was honored with the Presidential Medal of Freedom by Barack Obama and in 2017, Tyson appeared in director Richard Linklater's film Last Flag Flying, an adaptation from the 2005 novel of the same name.

2018 - 2020

In 2018, the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences announced that Tyson would receive an honorary Academy Award and in November htat year, Tyson became the first African-American woman to receive an honorary Oscar. She was also inducted into the American Theater Hall of Fame in the same year. In 2020, Cicely Tyson was chosen to be inducted into the Television Academy's Hall of Fame. Shortly after, she won Career Achievement Peabody award.

“I have managed Miss Tyson’s career for over 40 years, and each year was a privilege and blessing. Cicely thought of her new memoir as a Christmas tree decorated with all the ornaments of her personal and professional life. The day she died, she placed the last ornament, a Star, on top of the tree.”
- Larry Thompson
